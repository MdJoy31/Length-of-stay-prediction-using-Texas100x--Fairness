P0: Table X. Comparative Analysis of Methodological Approaches in Fairness-Aware Clinical Prediction
P1: Focus: Evaluation process, fairness framework, reliability methodology, and unaddressed limitations
P2: Note: Categories organised by methodological scope: (A) LOS prediction without fairness evaluation, (B) LOS prediction with fairness evaluation, (C) fairness metric stability studies from non-clinical domains, (D) healthcare fairness studies on non-LOS tasks, (E) this study. Columns emphasise evaluation process and identified methodological limitations rather than dataset characteristics or publication venue.
P4: Methodological Gap Analysis
P5: The comparison above reveals five systematic methodological gaps in the existing literature on fairness-aware clinical prediction:
P6: Gap 1: LOS prediction pipelines omit fairness evaluation entirely
P7: Among LOS prediction studies (Category A), none integrates fairness metrics into the evaluation pipeline. Even when interpretability tools such as SHAP are employed (Jain et al., 2024), researchers assess feature importance at the population level without disaggregating prediction errors across protected groups. Methodological implication: Low global feature importance of a demographic variable does not preclude disparate error rates conditioned on that variable. A model can exhibit group-level prediction bias even when demographic features carry negligible aggregate SHAP values.
P8: Gap 2: Fairness evaluation relies on point estimates without reliability quantification
P9: Among studies that do compute fairness metrics (Category B), all report single-point estimatesΓÇösometimes with bootstrap confidence intervals (Pfohl et al., 2021) but never with systematic stability assessment across resampled data subsets or varying sample sizes. Methodological implication: A fairness metric value computed on a single train/test split may not generalise. Without quantifying how much the metric fluctuates under resampling, practitioners cannot distinguish genuine disparities from statistical noiseΓÇöparticularly for underrepresented subgroups with limited sample sizes.
P10: Gap 3: Fairness reliability research has not been validated in clinical settings
P11: The fairness stability literature (Category C) provides strong evidence that DL nondeterminism (Qian et al., 2021), data reshuffling (Ganesh et al., 2023), and prediction arbitrariness (Cooper et al., 2024) produce substantial fairness metric variance. However, all three studies operate exclusively on non-clinical datasets (CelebA, Adult, COMPAS, HMDA). Methodological implication: Clinical EHR data exhibits characteristics absent from standard benchmarksΓÇöstructured missingness, severe class imbalance, correlated protected attributes (e.g., race correlates with insurance type), and hierarchical structure (patients nested within hospitals). Whether fairness variance findings transfer to this data regime is an open empirical question.
P12: Gap 4: Cross-site fairness heterogeneity is aggregated away
P13: Multi-site studies exist (Pfohl et al., 2021; Poulain et al., 2023; R├╢├╢sli et al., 2022) but report fairness at the database or federation level, not at the individual hospital level. Federated learning (Poulain et al., 2023) inherently trains across 208 hospital sites but aggregates fairness metrics, masking between-site heterogeneity. Methodological implication: A hospital adopting a model validated as ΓÇ£fairΓÇ¥ in aggregate may unknowingly deploy a locally unfair system. Site-level fairness assessment requires disaggregated cross-hospital analysisΓÇöwhich no existing study provides at scale.
P14: Gap 5: No empirical guidance on minimum sample sizes for reliable fairness assessment
P15: Singh & Chunara (2023) derive theoretical sample size formulas for fairness audits, but these have not been empirically validated on real clinical data. No existing study provides a data-driven answer to the question: ΓÇ£How many patients do I need before my fairness assessment is trustworthy?ΓÇ¥ Methodological implication: Without such guidance, hospitals with limited patient volumes cannot determine whether their fairness audits are statistically meaningful. This is particularly consequential for rare subgroups (e.g., Indigenous patients, non-binary individuals) where sample sizes are inherently small.
P16: How This Study Addresses These Gaps
P17: This study is the first to unify LOS prediction (Gap 1) with comprehensive fairness evaluation using 7 complementary metrics across 4 protected attributes (Gap 2), systematic reliability analysis via random-subset resampling and sample-size sensitivity testing (Gap 3), cross-hospital fairness validation across 441 hospitals at the individual-site level (Gap 4), and empirical minimum sample size recommendations demonstrating that N > 50,000 is required before fairness metrics stabilise below CV < 5% (Gap 5). By addressing all five methodological gaps simultaneously, this work provides both a diagnostic framework for evaluating the trustworthiness of fairness assessments and actionable guidance for clinical deployment.

Abstract
Among various aspects of ensuring the responsible design of AI
tools for healthcare applications, addressing fairness concerns has
been a key focus area. Specifically, given the wide spread of elec-
tronic health record (EHR) data and their huge potential to inform
a wide range of clinical decision support tasks, improving fairness
in this category of health AI tools is of key importance. While
such a broad problem (mitigating fairness in EHR-based AI models)
has been tackled using various methods, task- and model-agnostic
methods are rare. In this study, we aimed to target this gap by pre-
senting a new pipeline that generates synthetic EHR data, which
is not only consistent with (faithful to) the real EHR data but also
can reduce the fairness concerns (defined by the end-user) in the
downstream tasks, when combined with the real data. We demon-
strate the effectiveness of our proposed pipeline across various
downstream tasks and two different EHR datasets. Our proposed
pipeline can add a widely applicable and complementary tool to
the existing toolbox of methods to address fairness in health AI
applications, such as those modifying the design of a downstream
model.1.

2.1
Fairness in Machine Learning
Fairness within ML can be viewed in various ways. One common
way relates to categorizing fairness into two primary types: (i)
group fairness [8], and (ii) individual fairness [13]. Our focus in this
research is primarily on group fairness, which involves assessing
the discrepancy in performance across the groups (including the
protected and privileged groups). This emphasis aims to highlight
systemic biases against specific groups. As defined in the literature,
group fairness exhibits diverse interpretations, often selected based
on stakeholdersΓÇÖ perspectives and, at times, presenting conflicting
behaviors in specific contexts [24, 27].

rize the methods to mitigate bias in the ML community includes (i)
pre-processing techniques, which modify input data to eliminate
information correlated with sensitive attributes; (ii) in-processing
methods, which impose fairness constraints during the model learn-
ing process; and (iii) post-processing approaches, which adjust
model predictions after training [32]. Our research will process the
data and optimize the generator model for fairness.

There has been a growing interest in recent years in correctly predicting the Length of

ABSTRACT
Introduction: Prolonged Hospital Length of Stay (PLOS) is an indicator of deteriorated efficiency 
in Quality of Care. One goal of public health management is to reduce PLOS by identifying its 
most relevant predictors. The objective of this study is to explore Machine Learning (ML) models 
that best predict PLOS. 
Methods: Our dataset was collected from the French Medico-Administrative database (PMSI) as 
a retrospective cohort study of all discharges in the year 2015 from a large university hospital in 
France (APHM). The study outcomes were LOS transformed into a binary variable (long vs. short 
LOS) according to the 90th percentile (14 days). Logistic regression (LR), classification and regres┬¡
sion trees (CART), random forest (RF), gradient boosting (GB) and neural networks (NN) were 
applied to the collected data. The predictive performance of the models was evaluated using the 
area under the ROC curve (AUC). 
Results: Our analysis included 73,182 hospitalizations, of which 7,341 (10.0%) led to PLOS. The GB 
classifier was the most performant model with the highest AUC (0.810), superior to all the other 
models (all p-values <0.0001). The performance of the RF, GB and NN models (AUC ranged from 
0.808 to 0.810) was superior to that of the LR model (AUC = 0.795); all p-values <0.0001. In 
contrast, LR was superior to CART (AUC = 0.786), p < 0.0001. The variable most predictive of the 
PLOS was the destination of the patient after hospitalization to other institutions. The typical 
clinical profile of these patients (17.5% of the sample) was the elderly patient, admitted in 
emergency, for a trauma, a neurological or a cardiovascular pathology, more often institutiona┬¡
lized, with more comorbidities notably mental health problems, dementia and hemiplegia. 
Discussion: The integration of ML, particularly the GB algorithm, may be useful for health-care 
professionals and bed managers to better identify patients at risk of PLOS. These findings 
underscore the need to strengthen hospitals through targeted allocation to meet the needs of 
an aging population.
